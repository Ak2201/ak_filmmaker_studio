How to use Volume I.

Twelve steps, in order. Each step builds on the one before it. Don't skip ahead — the sequence is the method.

  1. Work in order. Step 1 is always the spark. By Step 12 you have a scene-by-scene outline ready to draft.
  2. Read the example before you write. Every step shows how Vikram Vedha, 96, and Por Thozhil answered the same question — first in English, then explained in Tanglish for clearer feel.
  3. Answer in your own words first. Write the messy version. Polish later.
  4. Tick the checks at the bottom of each step only when you genuinely believe you've nailed it.
  5. Save often. The page autosaves. Use EXPORT before you close.
  6. Print when done. Each step lands on its own page.
A NOTE ON RHYTHM

Steps 1–4 might take an evening. Step 8 (the 15-beat outline) might take a week. Step 11 (the scene list) might take a month. The blueprint is a guide, not a deadline.Konjam steps oru saayangaalathula mudichidalam, sila steps ku oru vaaram aagum, scene list ku oru maasame aagalaam. INTERLUDE · BETWEEN STEPS 02 AND 03

The Treatment Ladder.

Five rungs from one sentence to a step outline. Each rung is a real document with a target length. Producers and collaborators ask for these in this order. Build them in order, and the script writes itself.

FORMULA

Each rung must be readable on its own. If you can't show a rung independently and have it work, the rung above it is also broken.

Tanglish: ovvoru rung um thaniya padichaalum sense aaganum. Oru rung break aagudhuna, adhukku mela irukura rung um break-thaan.

Rung 1 — The Logline

5 – 30 words · already done in Step 02

If you can't fit it in one sentence, you don't yet know what your film is.

Rung 2 — One-Paragraph Synopsis

100 – 150 words · the elevator pitch

A producer reads this in 30 seconds. If they're not reaching for the next page, fix this paragraph.

Rung 3 — One-Pager

~ 400 – 500 words · one printed page

This is the document you send to a star's manager when they ask "what's it about?". Polish accordingly.

Rung 4 — The Treatment

5 – 12 pages · prose narrative of the whole film

If you can write a great treatment, you can write a great script. The treatment is where the structural problems show first — and where they're cheapest to fix.

Rung 5 — Step Outline

15 – 40 numbered scenes · the bridge to script

When you can read your step outline aloud and "feel" the film's shape — that's when you start writing pages.

Tip: Don't move to the next rung until the current one is honest. A weak treatment exposes a weak logline. A weak step outline exposes a weak treatment. The ladder forces the truth.

The Pre-Production
Blueprint.

From a locked script to "ROLL CAMERA" — twelve guided steps for direction, design, and the discipline of pre-production.

CURATED BY ARUNAK

How to use Volume II.

Pre-production is where most films secretly succeed or fail. The script is locked. Now every decision must serve the story.

  1. The script must be locked first. Don't start pre-production with an unfinished screenplay.
  2. Work in order — but loop back often. Casting affects costume. Locations affect cinematography. Decisions cascade.
  3. Every department must read the Director's Vision (Step 14). If your DOP and costume designer are reading three different visions, the film is already three films.
  4. Read the Por Thozhil craft lesson before you fill the fields.
  5. Save and export weekly. Pre-production runs months.
  6. Print sections and hand to HODs. Schedule to AD, lookbook to DOP, budget to producer.
A NOTE ON SCALE

This works for a Rs. 5 lakh short and a Rs. 5 crore feature. The questions are the same — only the answers scale up.Rs. 5 lakh short ku adhuvey, Rs. 5 crore feature ku adhuvey velai seyyum. Kelvigal same — answers thaan scale aagum.

The Glossary.

Every film term used in this blueprint, grouped by department. Bookmark this page — you'll come back to it whenever an HOD uses a word you don't recognise.

CAMERA & LENS
FOCAL LENGTH (mm)
How "wide" or "tight" a lens sees. Lower mm = wider field. 24mm is wide, 50mm is "normal" (close to human eye), 85mm is portrait, 200mm is tele.
ANAMORPHIC
A lens that squeezes a wider image onto the sensor; gives 2.39:1 scope, oval bokeh, and horizontal flares. Iconic Tamil/Indian "cinematic" feel.
PROBE / MACRO
Speciality lenses for extreme close-ups (food, eyes, insects). Probe lenses can shoot inside very small spaces.
ASPECT RATIO
The width:height of the frame. 1.85:1 is "flat" cinema, 2.39:1 is "scope" / CinemaScope, 16:9 is TV, 9:16 is vertical/reels.
FRAME RATE (FPS)
Frames-per-second. 24 fps is the cinema standard. 25 fps is PAL/India broadcast. Higher fps (60, 120) is for slow motion.
RESOLUTION
How many pixels in the frame. 4K is roughly 4 times HD. Higher = more detail, but also more storage and longer post-production.
SHOT & COVERAGE TYPES
MASTER
The wide shot covering the entire scene from start to finish. Editor cuts away from the master to closer shots and back.
WS / EWS
Wide Shot / Extreme Wide Shot. Sets location and scale. Character may be tiny in frame.
MS / MCU
Medium Shot (waist up) / Medium Close-Up (chest up). Conversational distance.
CU / ECU
Close-Up (face fills frame) / Extreme Close-Up (eyes, lips, an object). Reveals emotion or detail.
OS
Over-the-Shoulder shot. Camera looks past one character at another. The standard of dialogue scenes.
POV
Point-of-View shot — the camera is what a character is seeing. Used sparingly for impact.
2-SHOT
Two characters in frame together. Used to establish relationship and proximity.
INSERT / CUTAWAY
A close-up of an object (insert) or a brief glance away from the main action (cutaway). Used to emphasize or to give the editor a cut point.
CAMERA MOVEMENT
PAN / TILT
Pan = camera rotates horizontally on a fixed point. Tilt = vertical rotation. Camera doesn't move in space.
DOLLY
The whole camera moves on a wheeled platform. Dolly in = toward subject. Dolly out = away. Dolly with = beside a moving subject.
STEADICAM / GIMBAL
Stabilised handheld rigs. Steadicam is a body-worn mechanical rig; gimbal is electronic. Both let camera move smoothly without dolly tracks.
HANDHELD
Operator holds the camera with no stabilisation. Adds urgency, intimacy, immediacy. Documentary feel.
CRANE / JIB
Camera mounted on an arm that rises, falls, swoops. For grand reveals and overhead movement.
WHIP PAN
An ultra-fast pan, often used as a transition between scenes (e.g. action cinema or stylised cuts).
PRODUCTION
HOD
Head of Department. Each major area (camera, art, costume, sound) has one. The director collaborates with HODs, not every individual crew member.
1ST AD
First Assistant Director. The director's right hand on set. Owns the schedule, runs the floor, calls "rolling."
DOP / DP
Director of Photography / Cinematographer. Runs the camera and lighting departments. Translates the director's vision into images.
GAFFER
Chief lighting technician. Reports to the DOP. Plans and executes the lighting design.
RECCE
Reconnaissance — visiting locations before shoot. Tech recce = full HOD walkthrough at the time of day you'll actually shoot.
CALL SHEET
The daily schedule sent to crew the night before — call times, locations, scenes, cast required, weather, contacts. The sacred document.
DAY-OUT-OF-DAYS
A document showing which actors and crew are needed on which days. Drives the schedule and the contracts.
DOOD / DAY-PLAYER
An actor/crew member booked for a single day or short stretch, not the full shoot.
BUDGET
ATL — Above-the-line
Costs locked before production: writer, director, producer, lead cast. The "names" on the poster.
BTL — Below-the-line
Everything else: crew daily rates, equipment, locations, transport, food, insurance.
POST
Post-production costs: edit, sound mix, music, color grade, VFX, deliverables.
CONTINGENCY
The reserve fund (usually 10–15%) for the unexpected. Weather days, equipment failure, illness. Don't shoot without one.
SOUND
DIEGETIC
Sound that exists inside the world of the film — characters can hear it (a radio in the room, footsteps).
NON-DIEGETIC
Sound only the audience hears — background score, voiceover, sound design that comments on the action.
FOLEY
Reproduced everyday sounds (footsteps, fabric, doors) recorded in post by Foley artists matched to picture.
5.1 / 7.1 / ATMOS
Surround mix formats. 5.1 = 5 speakers + sub. 7.1 = 7 + sub. Atmos = object-based 3D sound including overhead.
SCRATCH TRACK
A temporary score (often a famous film's music) used during edit so the team can feel rhythm before the real composer delivers.
SCREENWRITING
SLUG / SLUGLINE
The first line of every scene: INT. KITCHEN — NIGHT. Tells the reader where and when.
BEAT
A unit of dramatic action — a turning point or shift. The 15-beat structure breaks a film into its essential beats.
CHARGE
The emotional value of a scene. If a scene starts negative and ends positive (or vice versa), the charge has flipped — that's drama.
SETUP / PAYOFF
Setup = a detail planted earlier (subtly). Payoff = when it returns and means more. Distance + reframing = power.
LOGLINE
The film in one sentence — usually under 30 words, with inciting event, protagonist, goal, obstacle, stakes.
WANT vs NEED
Want = what the protagonist consciously chases. Need = what they actually require to be whole. The arc is the gap between them.
