HOW TO USE THIS BLUEPRINT

One idea, told cleanly.

A short film isn't a short feature. It's a different form, with different rules. Where a feature gives the audience two hours to settle into the world, a short has to land in three minutes — sometimes one. The whole craft is in compression: one moment, one turn, one final image that earns the runtime.

Work through the eleven steps in order. Step 7 is where you actually write the script — the structured editor formats it like a real screenplay and Step 8 exports it as Fountain (the universal screenplay format that imports into Final Draft, WriterDuet, Highland, and most other apps). The whole thing saves locally to your browser; click JSON to back up.

SHORT FILM

  • One central moment — a turn, a reveal, a small but decisive change
  • One protagonist with one want
  • One or two locations
  • 5–30 minutes (festival sweet spot is 8–15)
  • Ends on a single image that contains the meaning
  • Festival calling card — your "business card" as a director

FEATURE FILM

  • Multiple moments / acts — a full character arc
  • Protagonist + antagonist + supporting cast
  • Multiple locations
  • 90–180 minutes
  • Ends on a transformed character
  • A theatrical / OTT product to be sold and distributed
01
The Seed.
~ ONE EVENING
EMPTY

Every short begins with one indivisible thing. An image. A moment. A question. A line you overheard. Find it before you write a frame.

FORMULA

If you can't say what your short is in one image or one sentence, you don't have a short — you have a feature in disguise. The seed is what the audience will remember six months later. Find that thing first.

Sirukadhai aarambichu mudikkardhukku munnadi, oru imageaaha, oru kelvi-aaga, oru moment-aaga athan core ah pidikko. Adhuvey oru year kazhichi audience nyabakam vechikkurathu. Adha munnadi find pannu.

WHY THIS MATTERS

Most failed shorts try to be tiny features — too many characters, too many subplots, too many ideas. The successful ones are confident enough to be about one thing. The seed is what gives you permission to cut everything that isn't it.

SEED · IMAGE
Pixar's Piper (2016)

A tiny sandpiper learns to feed itself when the tide comes in. The seed: a baby bird overcoming fear of water. Everything in the film is just that image, expanded.

SEED · MOMENT
Wes Anderson's Hotel Chevalier (2007)

Two ex-lovers meet in a Paris hotel room. The seed: the strange ritual of seeing someone you loved after you've stopped loving them. Thirteen minutes, one room.

The Seed — in one sentence Don't pitch the plot. Describe the single moment, image, or question that won't leave you alone.
The Image — what would you put on the poster? If your short had a single still that captured everything, what would it be?
Why this, why you, why now? If you don't know why you have to make THIS short, the audience won't either.

BEFORE YOU MOVE ON

  • My seed is one indivisible thing. Not three things stitched together.
  • I can describe it in a single sentence without losing the punch.
  • I have a clear mental image — I could draw it.
  • I know why I, specifically, am drawn to this seed.
02
The Logline.
~ TWO EVENINGS
EMPTY

A short film logline is even tighter than a feature's. Twenty-five words or less. Festival selectors read hundreds. Yours has to land in eight seconds.

RELATED: FEATURE · Step 02 Logline LIBRARY · Logline films
FORMULA

WHEN [inciting moment], a [specific protagonist] must [concrete action] before [cost / deadline] — or [specific consequence].

Trim until it can be said on one breath. If you have to add "and also" or "but then", you have two films.

Sirukadhai logline 25 words ku mela poga koodathu. "Adhukku mela", "appuram", "mathiri" ipdi extra clauses irundha — adhu rendu padam.

WHY THIS MATTERS

Festival programmers receive 5,000+ short film submissions a year. Your logline is the first 25 words that decide whether they watch the file. It's also the line you'll repeat 200 times in the next year — to actors, crew, festivals, journalists. Make it crisp.

EXAMPLE · 21 WORDS
What a tight logline looks like

"On the morning he leaves home for good, a divorced father teaches his eight-year-old daughter to ride a bicycle."

EXAMPLE · 19 WORDS
Conflict made concrete

"A night-shift cleaner finds a winning lottery ticket on a rich woman's desk and has thirty minutes to decide."

Draft 1 — The first attempt (will be wrong) Write it long. Don't worry about word count yet. Get the shape down.
Draft 2 — Cut every word that isn't load-bearing Adjectives are usually first to go. So is "in order to" → "to". So is the second clause.
Draft 3 — The locked logline ≤ 25 words. Specific. Active verbs. One protagonist, one want, one obstacle. Word counter below.
0 / 25 words

LOGLINE LOCK CHECK

  • 25 words or fewer.
  • No "and", no "but then", no second clause.
  • A stranger could understand my film from this sentence.
  • I can say it without checking notes.
03
The Character.
~ TWO EVENINGS
EMPTY

One protagonist. One want. One obstacle. That's all the runtime allows. Anything more and the audience can't hold it.

RELATED: FEATURE · Step 04 Protagonist FEATURE · Step 05 Antagonist
FORMULA

SHORT-FORM CHARACTER = ONE WANT + ONE OBSTACLE + ONE TURN. The "want" must be visible, concrete, and impossible to summarize as "happiness" or "love." The obstacle must be specific. The turn is what flips the want into something else — or impossible.

Sirukadhai character ku — oru want, oru thaduppu, oru turn. Want vague aaga irukkakoodaadhu — "santosham" mathiri abstract illa, concrete ah irukanum.

WHY THIS MATTERS

Features can build character through dozens of scenes. A short has 5–15 minutes. Your character has to be readable in their first 30 seconds — through choices, not through dialogue. The one-want / one-obstacle structure is what lets you skip the setup a feature would need.

Name & one-line description
The ONE want — concrete and visible Not "to feel valued." Something an actor can play. Something a camera can see.
The ONE obstacle Internal (their own flaw, fear, history) OR external (someone, something, time). Pick one. Don't dilute.
What we LEARN about them — without dialogue Three details an actor and director can show. Body language, an object they carry, how they enter a room.
The TURN — the moment that changes everything In a feature, this might be the midpoint. In a short, it's almost the whole film. What pivots in this character?

CHARACTER LOCK CHECK

  • My protagonist's want is concrete enough to play.
  • There is exactly ONE obstacle — not three.
  • The audience can read who they are in 30 seconds without exposition.
  • There is a clear turn — a moment of real change.
04
The 5-Beat Structure.
~ TWO EVENINGS
EMPTY

Forget the 15-beat feature structure. A short has five: Setup, Disturbance, Escalation, Turn, Image. Each beat earns its place or it's cut.

RELATED: FEATURE · Step 08 The 15 Beats FEATURE · Setups & Payoffs
FORMULA · 5 BEATS

1. Setup (≤15% of runtime) — Establish character, world, normal.
2. Disturbance (~10%) — The thing that breaks the normal.
3. Escalation (~50%) — The pursuit of the want; obstacles compound.
4. Turn (~15%) — The moment everything changes.
5. Image (~10%) — The final frame that holds the meaning.

Sirukadhai 5 beat ah ottum: setup → disturbance → escalation → turn → image. Ovvoru beat um earn pannanum — sumaa irukkamudiyaadhu.

WHY THIS MATTERS

The 5-beat is the minimum a short film needs to feel like a story rather than a sketch. Without all five, audiences sense something is missing — even if they can't name it. The most common short film failure is missing beat 5: a turn happens but the film doesn't land on a final image that contains the meaning.

Setup

BEAT 2 · ~10%

Disturbance

BEAT 3 · ~50%

Escalation

BEAT 4 · ~15%

Turn

BEAT 5 · ~10%

Image

Self-test — Read the five beats aloud back-to-back. Does it feel like a story?

STRUCTURE LOCK CHECK

  • Each of the five beats is filled in.
  • The escalation beat earns its 50% — there's actual rising tension.
  • The turn isn't where the story ends — there's a final image after.
  • The final image contains the film's meaning.
05
The World.
~ ONE EVENING
EMPTY

For a short, the world is usually one location, one time of day, one weather. The constraints aren't compromises — they're the form's strength.

RELATED: FEATURE · Step 07 The World FEATURE · Step 15 Lookbook
FORMULA

The shorter the film, the more loaded each location must be. A single-location short is not a limitation — it forces every piece of the frame to do double duty. The chair the character sits in becomes a character. The light through the window becomes a clock.

Sirukadhai oru location la nadakum — adhu compromise illa, adhuvey form oda strength. Each prop, each frame extra meaning kudukum.

SINGLE LOCATION
Locke (2013) — feature, but the principle holds

One man, one car, one phone. The constraint forced every choice to mean something. The short film equivalent: one room, one street, one café.

DUAL LOCATION
Wes Anderson's Hotel Chevalier

Hotel room and balcony. The balcony is just three steps from the room — but it's where the conversation can't happen indoors.

Primary location — describe it like a character
Time of day & weather Pick the one that loads the scene with maximum meaning. Dawn ≠ Noon ≠ Dusk. Rain ≠ Heat.
Three sensory anchors — sound, texture, recurring object
Rules the world establishes (3–5) What's allowed, what's forbidden, what's normal here. Even a realist short has rules.

WORLD LOCK CHECK

  • My primary location is specific and loaded with meaning.
  • Time of day and weather are deliberate, not default.
  • There are 2–3 sensory anchors I can keep returning to.
  • If I had only this one location, the script still works.
06
The Scene Map.
~ TWO EVENINGS
EMPTY

Most shorts work with 5 to 12 scenes. List them before you write a line of dialogue. Each scene must do at least one of three jobs: reveal character, advance want, or set up the turn.

FORMULA

For each scene, ask: WHO is in it? What do they WANT? What's the OBSTACLE? How does it END differently from how it started? If a scene has no answer to all four, cut it.

Ovvoru scene um — yaaru, enna want, enna thaduppu, ennapadi muddiyum. Ipdi naalu kelvi answer aagatha scene-a vetti vidu.

WHY THIS MATTERS

You're going to spend most of your runtime on the escalation beat (Beat 3). That's where the scene map matters most. A weak escalation = scenes that stall. A strong escalation = each scene tighter than the last, each one closer to the turn.

Self-test: read the "What happens" column top-to-bottom Do the scenes escalate? Does each one push the protagonist closer to the turn? If you read the scene map without dialogue, does it still feel like a story?

SCENE MAP LOCK CHECK

  • 5 to 12 scenes total. (Anything more is feature territory.)
  • Each scene has a clear who-want-obstacle-end answer.
  • The escalation builds — scenes get tighter, not slower.
  • No two scenes do the same job.

09
Production Reality.
~ TWO EVENINGS
EMPTY

Most shorts shoot in 1–4 days, with 3–6 crew, in 1–3 locations. Knowing your reality before you finalize the script lets you cut what you can't actually shoot.

RELATED: FEATURE · Step 23 Schedule & Budget LIBRARY · Equipment Estimator
FORMULA

BUDGET = (DAYS × CREW SIZE × LOCATION FEE) + EQUIPMENT + POST. For a serious calling-card short in Chennai, ₹50K–₹3L is typical. Festival-quality with name talent: ₹5L–₹15L. Your script must fit your budget, not the other way around.

Sirukadhai budget = days × crew × location + equipment + post. Chennai la calling-card short — ₹50K-₹3L common. Script ah budget kku fit panna vendiyathu, vice versa illa.

WHY THIS MATTERS

Many first-time shorts fail in pre-production because the script demands things the budget can't deliver — drone shots, child actors, period costumes, night exteriors. Cut those before script lock. A great cheap short beats a compromised expensive one every time.

Five things in the script I cannot afford to shoot Drone? Crowd scenes? Children under 12? Period props? Animals? Be honest. Cut them now or find workarounds.
My biggest production risk Weather? Child actor availability? One key location permission? Be honest with yourself.

PRODUCTION REALITY CHECK

  • My budget covers the script as written. No magical thinking.
  • I know what I'm cutting if money runs out (priority list).
  • Locations are confirmed (or I have a clear path to confirming).
  • Cast is locked or in serious negotiation.
10
Festival Strategy.
~ ONE EVENING
EMPTY

A short film is a calling card. Festivals are how it gets seen. Strategy: pick 12–20 festivals across three tiers, submit early, never online-premiere before a top-tier rejection.

FORMULA · THE FESTIVAL HIERARCHY

TIER 1 (Oscar-qualifying / A-list): Cannes, Sundance, Berlinale, TIFF, Locarno, Venice, SXSW, Tribeca. Submit FIRST. They have first-screening rules — your film cannot have premiered online or at any other festival before them.
TIER 2 (Regional A-list / strong): Clermont-Ferrand, Palm Springs ShortFest, Aspen, IFFI Goa, MAMI Mumbai, IFFK Kerala. Submit AFTER Tier 1 results.
TIER 3 (Genre / regional / online): Fantasia, Sitges, Dharamshala, Bengaluru International, Vimeo Staff Picks, NoBudge, Short of the Week.

Festival ah 3-tier la divide pannu. Tier 1 munadi submit pannu — onlina ya ettungalla mela istha mudiyaadhu. Tier 1 result vandha apparam tier 2, then tier 3.

WHY THIS MATTERS

The single biggest mistake first-time filmmakers make: posting their short on YouTube the day it's done. That kills your shot at every premiere-restricted festival — which is most of the top ones. Online release is the LAST step, after your festival run, not the first.

11
The Final Lock.
~ 30 MIN
EMPTY

Read aloud, double-check, and lock. After this, the script doesn't change unless you discover something on set.

FORMULA

The locked script is a contract — with yourself, your cast, your crew, and your future editor. Until it's locked, you're still in writing mode. Once it's locked, you're in production mode. The two cannot coexist.

Lock aana script oru contract — un, cast, crew, editor odu. Lock aagama irundha indha rendu mode um onnaaga nadakkaadhu. Lock aanaa puri vendam — production start.

FINAL LOCK CHECKLIST · 20 ITEMS

  • My logline lands in 25 words or fewer.
  • My protagonist has one clear want.
  • There is exactly one obstacle.
  • There is one decisive turn.
  • There is one final image that holds the meaning.
  • All five beats are present and earned.
  • The escalation builds; no scene stalls.
  • No two scenes do the same job.
  • Page count fits target runtime (1 page ≈ 1 minute).
  • No unfilmable elements (drone shot I can't afford, child I haven't cast, etc.).
  • All locations are practical and confirmed (or I have plan B).
  • Cast is locked or in advanced negotiation.
  • Budget covers the script as written.
  • I've read the script aloud, scene by scene.
  • A trusted first reader has given notes.
  • I've cut every line that doesn't earn its place.
  • I've exported a Fountain backup.
  • My festival list is tiered and dated.
  • I have NOT released anything online yet.
  • I'm ready to call ROLL CAMERA.

The Short Film Glossary.

Every term used in this blueprint that a first-time filmmaker might not know. Bookmark; you'll come back when you hit a new collaborator's vocabulary.

SCREENPLAY FORMAT
SLUG LINE
The header above each scene. Format: INT. or EXT. — LOCATION — TIME OF DAY. INT. = interior, EXT. = exterior. Always ALL CAPS.
ACTION
Description of what happens, in present tense, third person. No camera directions in the script unless absolutely necessary.
PARENTHETICAL
A short instruction below a character name in italics, e.g. (whispering). Use sparingly — actors hate them.
FADE IN / FADE OUT
The opening and closing of the script. FADE IN: at the start, FADE OUT. at the end. Optional but traditional.
O.S. / O.C. / V.O.
Off Screen, Off Camera, Voice Over. Different effects: O.S. = in the scene but unseen; V.O. = narrating from outside.
FOUNTAIN
A plain-text screenplay format using markdown-like rules. Universal — opens in every pro screenwriting app. The export format used by this blueprint.
FINAL DRAFT
The most common professional screenwriting software. Imports Fountain. Often required by festivals and producers.
HIGHLAND / WRITERDUET / FADE IN
Final Draft alternatives. All open Fountain files. Highland is Mac-only; WriterDuet is web-based; Fade In is cross-platform.
PRODUCTION
CALL SHEET
The day's plan distributed to cast and crew. Times, locations, scenes to shoot, contact info. The 1st AD's main artifact.
RECCE
Location scouting. A "tech recce" is when DOP, sound, art and director walk the location pre-shoot to plan everything practically.
ROLL CAMERA
The director's call to start a take. Followed by ACTION, then CUT.
DCP
Digital Cinema Package — the professional theatrical screening format. Most major festivals require a DCP for projection. Costs ₹3K–₹15K to produce.
PRORES
Apple's professional video codec. ProRes 422 HQ or 4444 are the festival-standard delivery formats alongside DCP.
H.264 / H.265
Common compressed video codecs. H.264 in MP4 is the standard online preview format for festival submissions via FilmFreeway.
1st AD
First Assistant Director. Runs the floor. Calls "rolling," manages the schedule, protects the director's time. Critical role even on a 2-day short.
DOP / DP
Director of Photography / Cinematographer. Designs the look with the director. On a short, often also operates the camera.
FESTIVAL & SUBMISSION
FILMFREEWAY
The dominant submission platform. Most festivals (~80%) accept submissions through it. One profile, one upload, many submissions.
OSCAR-QUALIFYING
Festivals whose top awards make a short eligible for Academy Award consideration. Cannes, Sundance, Tribeca, SXSW, etc.
WORLD PREMIERE / INTL PREMIERE / NORTH AMERICAN PREMIERE
Festival hierarchy. Tier-1 festivals usually require world or country-tier premiere status. Posting on YouTube counts as a world premiere — and disqualifies you.
SCREENING FEE
Some festivals pay filmmakers a small honorarium for screening. ₹0 to ~$500 typical.
PRESS KIT / EPK
Electronic Press Kit. Includes synopsis, director's bio, film stills, poster, technical specs. Festivals require this with submission.
SAG / GUILD
U.S. actor unions. Most Indian shorts are non-union; relevant only if hiring U.S. talent.
CRAFT TERMS
SHOWING vs. TELLING
"Show, don't tell." A short film should reveal character through action and image, not exposition. Cut "as you know" dialogue ruthlessly.
SETUP / PAYOFF
An object, line, or detail introduced early ("setup") that returns later with new meaning ("payoff"). Compressed in shorts; often only one or two pairs.
SUBTEXT
What the scene is REALLY about, beneath what's being said. The best short film dialogue has subtext that lands harder than the surface meaning.
TURN
The single moment in a short where everything pivots. The midpoint of a feature in spirit; in a short, it's almost the whole film.
FINAL IMAGE
The last frame the audience sees. In a short film, this image must contain the meaning. If your film could lose its final image, you don't have a final image yet.
